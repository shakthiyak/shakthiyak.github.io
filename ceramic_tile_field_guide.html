New to the tile floor?

From raw powder to a rectified porcelain tile — everything a newcomer needs to know.

A practical field guide to ceramic tile manufacturing: the production flow, the product families, the machines on the floor, and the terms everyone around you already knows.

Product Families

Almost every tile on the floor belongs to one of two broad families. Which one depends on density, water absorption, and where the tile is meant to go.

Glazed Ceramic Tile

Wall & Floor Tile — Higher Porosity
  • Red or white body, more porous before glazing seals the surface
  • Typical products: wall tiles, light-traffic floor tiles
  • Water absorption class BIII / BIIb (per ISO 13006) — lighter, generally cheaper

Porcelain / Vitrified Tile

GVT (Glazed) & FBV (Full Body) — Low Porosity
  • Dense, vitrified body, water absorption under 0.5% (class BIa)
  • Typical products: heavy-traffic floors, outdoor paving, large-format slabs
  • Often rectified (precision-cut edges) for near-zero grout lines; FBV carries colour through the whole body so chips don't show

The Production Process

A single tile passes through around a dozen stages before it's ready to ship — including grading branches, reclaim loops, and returns. This reflects a typical fast-fire tile process chart, not a textbook simplification.

Main Departments

⛏️ Body Preparation Line

  1. Raw Material Grinding (Wet Ball Mill)
  2. Spray Drying

📦 Powder Line

  1. Powder Storage & Ageing (Silos)

🎨 Glaze Line

  1. Frit & Glaze Preparation
  2. Engobe Application
🔀Body Preparation feeds the Powder Line, which in turn feeds Pressing. Glaze Line feeds Engobe Application, which rejoins the main line at Glazing — and reclaimed overspray loops back to Frit & Glaze Preparation for reuse.
Main Line
1

Pressing

Aged powder from the silos is pressed at high pressure in a steel die (hydraulic press) to form the tile blank. Trim scrap and dust are reclaimed back to Raw Material Grinding (see Rework Loops below).

2

Drying (Vertical Dryer)

Removes residual moisture from the pressed tile before glazing, since any leftover moisture would cause defects when glaze hits the hot surface.

3

Bisque Inspection

The dried, unglazed tile is checked for cracks and dimension issues. It also branches off here to Engobe Application before rejoining the line.

4

Glazing

Glaze is applied by bell, disc, or waterfall application — fed by the main line and by the returning Engobe Application step.

5

Digital Decoration

Inkjet printing lays down the pattern or design on the glazed surface before firing.

6

Kiln Loading

Decorated tiles are loaded into the fast-fire roller kiln.

7

Kiln Unloading

Fired tiles are removed from the kiln, ready for sorting.

8

Sorting & Grading

Final sort into three outcomes: Grade 1, Grade 2, or Reject — see below.

After Sorting — Three Outcomes

✅ Grade 1 (Premium)

  1. Rectification & Polishing (for GVT/Lappato lines)
  2. → Warehouse

🔶 Grade 2 (Commercial)

  1. Quality Check (Caliber & Shade Sort)
  2. → Warehouse, packed and labelled as commercial grade

❌ Reject / Broken

  1. Crushed for grog recovery
  2. Disposed or sold as aggregate — does not re-enter the line
Rework Loops
♻️Pressing → Raw Material Grinding: unfired trim scrap and dust from pressing is reclaimed back into the wet mill instead of being wasted. Once a tile is fired and vitrified, though, it can no longer go back into slip — that's why Reject pieces are crushed for grog rather than reclaimed as raw material.
♻️Glazing ↔ Frit & Glaze Preparation: reclaimed overspray from the glazing line loops back into glaze preparation for reuse.
Warehouse, Packing & Returns
→

Warehouse

Receives Grade 1 and Grade 2 stock, plus Delivery Orders from Sales.

→

Packing

Orders are packed for dispatch — often "nested" with matching batch/shade numbers so a room's tiles come from one firing.

→

Shipping

Packed goods are dispatched to the customer.

↩️Customer Return → Inspection: returned goods are inspected — good condition items return to Warehouse stock, bad condition items are disposed.

Machinery You'll Hear Named

Equipment brand names get used like common nouns on the floor — worth knowing what each one actually does.

SACMI

Italian manufacturer best known in this industry for spray dryers and hydraulic presses — the machines that turn slurry into powder and powder into a pressed tile blank.

SITI B&T / System Ceramics

Associated with kilns and complete glazing lines — the equipment that fires the ware and applies glaze with consistent, repeatable coverage.

Durst / EFI Cretaprint

Names that come up around digital inkjet decoration — the printers that lay down the pattern or design on the tile surface before firing.

Barbieri

Associated with spectrophotometer-based shade sorting — automated equipment that groups finished tiles into consistent colour batches.

Roller Kiln (Fast-Fire)

Not a brand — the firing furnace itself. Tiles ride on rollers through a single, fast firing cycle (often under an hour), unlike the slower shuttle kilns used elsewhere in ceramics.

Machine names and their exact roles vary by plant and supplier contracts — treat these as general orientation, not a substitute for your own factory's equipment list.

Common Defects

The defects QC is actually looking for at Bisque Inspection and Sorting & Grading — what they look like, and what usually causes them.

Pinholing

Tiny holes in the fired glaze surface, caused by trapped air or gas escaping through the glaze during firing. Caught at Sorting & Grading.

Crawling

Glaze pulls back and bunches up, leaving bare patches of exposed body. Usually caused by dust, grease, or moisture on the surface before spraying.

Crazing

A fine network of hairline cracks across the glaze surface, caused by a mismatch between how much the glaze and the body shrink during cooling.

Black Core (Black Coring)

A dark grey or black band left in the centre of the tile, from organic matter or sulphur not fully burning out before the fast firing cycle seals the surface.

Warping / Bowing

The tile loses its true flatness — often from uneven drying, uneven kiln heat, or inconsistent pressing density across the tile.

Lamination / Delamination

Layers separate inside the body, usually from air trapped in the powder during pressing that couldn't escape before the die closed.

Chipping / Edge Damage

Small chips at corners or edges from handling, mostly after firing when the tile is at its hardest but also most brittle.

Shade Variation

Colour differs slightly between tiles from the same or different batches, from small shifts in glaze application, firing temperature, or raw material lots.

Caliber (Size) Variation

The fired tile's actual size drifts from its nominal size, mainly from shrinkage variation. Sorted into caliber groups so a room's tiles fit together evenly.

Efflorescence

A whitish salt deposit that migrates to the surface, usually appearing after installation as moisture carries soluble salts from the body or adhesive outward.

Note: defect names and root causes can vary slightly by plant and by whoever trained the QC team — this is the general pattern used across the industry, not a substitute for your own factory's defect catalogue.

Glossary

Type to filter.

Slip / Slurry
Liquid clay-and-mineral mixture produced by wet ball milling, before it's spray dried into powder.
Spray Dried Powder
Fine, free-flowing granulate made by atomising slurry in a spray dryer — the feedstock for pressing.
Green Tile
A pressed but not-yet-fired tile, still fragile and reworkable.
Bisque
Dried, unglazed tile checked at Bisque Inspection before glaze and decoration are applied.
Engobe
An opaque base coat applied before the glaze, used to mask the body colour or improve glaze adhesion.
Frit
Pre-melted, powdered glass used as a base ingredient in glaze — melted and quenched in advance so the glaze fires predictably.
Digital / Inkjet Decoration
Printing the surface pattern with ceramic ink via inkjet heads, rather than screen printing or rotocolour rollers.
Rectified / Rectification
Tiles with edges precision-ground after firing to an exact, uniform size — allowing very thin grout lines.
Caliber / Calibration
The actual fired size group a tile falls into after shrinkage — tiles are sorted by caliber so a batch lays flush.
Shade / Tono
The colour batch a tile is sorted into — tiles from the same shade group are meant to be installed together.
Water Absorption Class
ISO 13006 grouping (BIa, BIb, BIIa, BIIb, BIII) ranking tiles from least porous (BIa, porcelain) to most porous (BIII, wall tile).
PEI Rating
A I–V scale (Porcelain Enamel Institute) rating a glazed tile's resistance to surface abrasion — higher numbers suit heavier foot traffic.
R-Rating
A slip-resistance rating (R9–R13) used mainly for floor and outdoor tiles, based on a ramp test.
Lappato
A semi-polished finish — the surface is lightly ground to bring out a soft sheen without full mirror polish.
GVT (Glazed Vitrified Tile)
A vitrified (low-porosity) body with a glazed surface on top — see Product Families above.
FBV (Full Body Vitrified)
A vitrified tile where colour and pattern run through the entire body, so chips and wear don't reveal a different colour underneath.
MOR (Modulus of Rupture)
A standard measure of a fired tile's breaking strength, tested by loading a sample until it fractures.
Nesting
Packing tiles of two related sizes together in the same box to fill space efficiently during shipping.
Grog
Crushed, pre-fired ceramic material recovered from rejects — reused as an aggregate rather than fed back into fresh slip.

Numbers You'll See Everywhere

These show up in planning sheets constantly — here's what they mean in plain terms.

★ Key Metric
Water Absorption %
How porous the fired body is
Sets the tile's classification (BIa–BIII) and which applications it's suitable for — floors, walls, or outdoor use.
Firing Shrinkage %
Green-to-fired size reduction
Die design must oversize the pattern to compensate, and it drives caliber sorting after firing.
MOR (Breaking Strength)
Load a fired tile withstands before fracture
A core structural spec, especially for large-format and floor tiles.
PEI Rating
Surface abrasion resistance, I–V
Determines whether a glazed tile is suited to light residential or heavy commercial foot traffic.

The Production Process

A single water closet or wash basin passes through around a dozen stages before it's ready to ship. Here's the order, start to finish.

01

Slip Preparation

Clay, feldspar, quartz and other minerals are blended with water into "slip" — a liquid clay body. It's ball-milled, sieved, and de-aired so it flows evenly into the mould.

02

Mould Making Case Arrange · Mould Making · Mould Drying

Plaster moulds are cast from master patterns, then dried before use. Moulds are porous on purpose — that porosity is what pulls water out of the slip during casting.

03

Casting

Slip is poured (or pressure-injected) into the mould. The plaster draws water from the slip against the mould wall, building up a clay layer. Once thick enough, excess slip is drained. High-volume plants often use pressure casting machines instead of gravity casting to cut cycle time.

04

Greenware Handling GWI

The unfired piece — called "greenware" — is removed from the mould. GWI (Green Ware Inspection) checks it for cracks, warping, or casting defects before it moves any further.

05

Fettling & Trap Glazing Trap Glaze

Seams and rough edges from the mould joints are smoothed. Hard-to-reach areas — like the internal trapway of a toilet — get glaze applied manually or robotically before the general spray, since the main glazing pass can't reach inside.

06

Drying

Greenware is dried to remove remaining moisture. Skipping or rushing this step is a common cause of cracking later in the kiln.

07

Glazing Spray

The dried piece is sprayed with glaze — a glass-forming coating that gives the finished piece its smooth, glossy surface. Larger plants use robotic spray booths for consistent coverage.

08

Kiln Loading & Firing Kiln

Glazed pieces are loaded onto kiln cars or setters and fired at roughly 1150–1250°C. This is where the clay body vitrifies (turns dense and glass-like) and the glaze melts into its final glossy finish.

09

Glost Inspection Glost

"Glost" ware means glazed-and-fired. This inspection checks the finished surface for pinholes, crawling, dimensional accuracy, and other post-firing defects — and sorts pieces into first quality vs. seconds.

10

Packing & Warehouse Warehouse

Approved pieces are packed and moved to the warehouse for dispatch to distributors, retailers, or project sites.

Note: exact stage names and sequence vary slightly by factory. This is the general shape of the process — your own plant's SOPs are always the source of truth.

Product Families

Almost everything on the floor is made from one of two clay bodies. Which one depends on the size, shape, and complexity of the piece.

VC

Vitreous China
  • Dense, low-porosity body once fired
  • Typical products: wash basins, pedestals, cisterns/tanks, lids, most modern water closets
  • Generally finer detail, smoother finish

FFC

Fine Fire Clay
  • Slightly more porous than VC before glazing seals it
  • Typical products: larger basins, kitchen/utility sinks, some water closets, bidets
  • Historically favoured for bigger or more complex shapes less prone to warping

Machinery You'll Hear Named

Equipment brand names get used like common nouns on the floor — worth knowing what each one actually does.

SACMI

Italian manufacturer best known in this industry for pressure-casting systems — machines that inject slip under pressure for a faster, more consistent cycle than traditional gravity casting into plaster moulds.

GAROLL

Associated with robotic glazing lines — automated spray booths that apply glaze with more consistent, repeatable coverage than manual spraying.

UNIMAC

Equipment supplier whose name comes up around handling and auxiliary systems on the production line. Exact scope varies by plant — worth confirming locally what your factory's UNIMAC units specifically do.

Kiln (Tunnel / Shuttle)

Not a brand — the firing furnace itself. Tunnel kilns move ware continuously through fixed heat zones; shuttle kilns fire one batch at a time in a chamber. Either way, this is where vitrification happens.

Machine names and their exact roles vary by plant and supplier contracts — treat these as general orientation, not a substitute for your own factory's equipment list.

Glossary

Type to filter.

Slip
Liquid clay body used for casting — clay and minerals suspended in water.
Greenware / GWI
Unfired ("green") ceramic ware, and the Green Ware Inspection step that checks it before further processing.
Trap Glaze
Glaze manually or robotically applied to hard-to-reach internal areas (like a WC trapway) before the main spray pass.
Spray
The main glaze application stage, typically done in a spray booth.
Glost
Ware that has been glazed and fired — "glost inspection" is the post-firing quality check.
Vitrification
The process during firing where the clay body becomes dense and glass-like, giving fired ceramic its strength and low porosity.
Shrinkage
The size reduction from wet clay to fired piece, from both drying and firing. Mould dimensions must account for it up front.
Firing Loss / Firing Yield
The proportion of material lost (or retained) during firing — used to work backward from a finished-piece target to how much green material must be cast.
VC (Vitreous China)
A dense, low-porosity ceramic body — see Product Families above.
FFC (Fine Fire Clay)
A ceramic body typically used for larger or more complex shapes — see Product Families above.
WIP (Work in Progress)
Pieces that have entered production but haven't yet reached finished/packed status.
AQL (Acceptable Quality Level)
A statistical sampling standard (ISO 2859) used to decide how many units from a batch to inspect, and how many defects are tolerable before rejecting the lot.
OEE (Overall Equipment Effectiveness)
A standard efficiency metric: Availability × Performance × Quality, used to measure how well a machine or kiln is actually being used.
ERP (Enterprise Resource Planning)
Company-wide software for production, inventory, and finance. Common systems in this industry include FACTS, xAct, Infos, and SAP.

Numbers You'll See Everywhere

These show up in planning sheets constantly — here's what they mean in plain terms.

Shrinkage %
Green-to-fired size reduction
Mould design must oversize the pattern to compensate.
Firing Loss %
Material lost during firing
Used to calculate how much must be cast to hit a finished-goods target.
Casting Yield
Good pieces per cast, after defects
Drives how many casting days a given order needs.
